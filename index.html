I. Duration: Full Day Workshop

The Neuromorphic Computing for Development and Learning Workshop is a full-day event focused on advancements in neuromorphic computing and developmental learning. It brings together experts, researchers, and industry professionals to explore neuromorphic sensing, computing with spiking neural networks (SNNs), and their role in developmental learning. Keynote talks and tutorials will cover event-driven sensing, visual navigation, contrastive learning, spike-based learning, and neuromorphic architectures. The event will include networking sessions, industry demonstrations, and hands-on tutorials on selective attention, perception, and embodied learning.

II. Content of the Workshop

Neuromorphic computing offers real-time, low-power solutions through event-driven architectures and SNNs, addressing continual learning and sensory-motor integration challenges. This workshop will explore experience-driven evolution, synaptic plasticity, local adaptation, and temporal coding. Topics link neuromorphic principles to developmental learning concepts such as self-organization, incremental learning, contrastive learning, and the embodiment of cognition. Hands-on sessions will demonstrate applications like selective attention, navigation with neuromorphic vision, and closed-loop sensorimotor learning.

NCDL Topics (including but not limited to):

  • Neural inspiration for learning systems: Synaptic plasticity vs. optimization algorithms
  • Bio-plausible backpropagation and local learning rules
  • Contrastive learning and surprise-driven learning (e.g., Wulfram Gerstner's work)
  • Continual learning and low-data learning
  • Fast learning: one-shot and few-shot learning
  • Neuromorphic computing for cognitive robotics
  • Neural cognitive architectures and embodied autonomy

III. Target Audience

This workshop targets researchers, students, and professionals interested in neuromorphic computing, developmental learning systems, robotics, and biologically inspired AI. It emphasizes interdisciplinary collaboration with neuroscience, psychology, and robotics communities to develop adaptive, autonomous systems.
